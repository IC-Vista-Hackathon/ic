# Michigan — Notification Law Reference

**Disclaimer:** Internal compliance reference only. Not legal advice. Verify with counsel before relying on any item below, especially anything marked "Not confirmed."

## 1. Data Breach Notification — Identity Theft Protection Act (MCL 445.61–445.79d)

- **Statute cite:** MCL 445.72 (notice requirements); definitions at MCL 445.63.
- **Trigger:** Person/agency that owns or licenses data in a database discovers a security breach and must notify each MI resident whose unencrypted, unredacted personal information was accessed and acquired by an unauthorized person — **unless** the entity determines the breach has not/is not likely to cause substantial loss or injury, or result in identity theft, to one or more residents.
- **Deadline to notify consumers:** No fixed day count — **"without unreasonable delay."** Confirmed current text. A delay is permitted only if necessary to determine the scope of the breach and restore the reasonable integrity of the database (or per law-enforcement request); notice must follow without unreasonable delay once that work is done.
- **AG notice:** MCL 445.72 does not impose an affirmative AG-notice duty; the Attorney General's role is enforcement (civil fines), not a mandatory recipient of breach notice. Not confirmed as a hard requirement — verify with counsel.
- **Consumer reporting agency (CRA) notice:** Required **"without unreasonable delay"** after resident notice, **unless** fewer than 1,000 residents were notified, or the entity is subject to federal banking-agency notification rules (15 U.S.C. §§ 6801–6809, GLBA). No specific hour/day count beyond "without unreasonable delay" — this is not a 48-hour rule (unlike Minnesota).
- **Payment card + security code as "personal information":** **Yes, explicitly.** MCL 445.63(r): first name/initial + last name combined with, among other elements, "financial account number OR credit or debit card number, combined with required security code, access code, or password."
- **Encryption safe harbor:** Yes. Notice duty attaches only to "unencrypted and unredacted personal information." MCL 445.63(g) defines "encrypted" as transformation via algorithmic process yielding low probability of assigning meaning without the confidential process/key, or other methods rendering data unreadable/unusable.
- **Good-faith employee exception:** Not a "security breach" if unauthorized access was by an employee/individual acting in good faith, in connection with job duties, and personal information was not further used or disclosed. (MCL 445.63(b))
- **Penalties:**
  - Criminal — knowingly/intentionally issuing a false breach notice: up to 93 days imprisonment and/or fines of $250 (1st offense), $500 (2nd), $750 (3rd+).
  - Civil — knowing failure to provide required notice: civil fine up to **$250 per violation**, aggregate liability capped at **$750,000 per security breach**. AG or a county prosecuting attorney brings the action.
- **Private right of action:** None stated in the statute; the penalty provisions note they don't displace other available civil remedies under state/federal law, but they do not themselves create a private cause of action. Not confirmed beyond this — verify with counsel.

## 2. Consumer-Facing Payment/Billing Notices (auto-renewal, price increase, cancellation confirmation)

- **Status: No enacted, general Michigan statute found.** House Bill 4826 (2025–2026 session, introduced 8/27/2025) would add automatic-renewal disclosure/notice requirements to the Michigan Consumer Protection Act, but **as of this research it has not passed** — it's pending in the House Economic Competitiveness Committee (roughly 25% progression per legislative tracking). Do not treat HB 4826's terms (14-pt disclosure, 30–60 day pre-renewal electronic notice for contracts >2 months, 30-day post-notice cancellation window, no cancellation fees) as current law.
- No Michigan-specific price-increase-notice or cancellation-confirmation statute was identified. **Not confirmed — verify with counsel** whether any sector-specific rule (insurance, telecom, utilities) applies to your product; general MCPA unfair/deceptive-practices provisions may still apply to auto-renewal practices even without a dedicated statute.

## 3. State EFT/ACH Notice Requirements Beyond Reg E

- No Michigan-specific statute establishing preauthorized-transfer notice or change-in-terms notice requirements beyond federal Reg E (12 CFR 1005.10) was located. Michigan's Money Transmission Services Act (MCL 487.1001 et seq., Act 250 of 2006) governs licensing of money transmitters, not consumer notice content for recurring EFT/ACH debits. **Not confirmed — verify with counsel** whether any MI Consumer Protection Act general provision layers additional notice duties.
