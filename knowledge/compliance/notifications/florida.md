# Florida — Notification Law Reference

**Disclaimer:** Internal compliance reference only. Not legal advice. Verify with counsel before relying on this for a specific decision.

## 1. Data Breach Notification — Florida Information Protection Act (FIPA)

- **Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 501.171.
- **Trigger:** Breach of security of a system maintained by a "covered entity" that compromises the confidentiality, integrity, or security of unencrypted/unredacted "personal information" (or the confidential process/key for encrypted data).
- **Consumer notice deadline:** As expeditiously as practicable, **no later than 30 days** after determination of the breach (or reason to believe one occurred). One **15-day extension** available with written good-cause notice to the Dept. of Legal Affairs within the original 30-day window — max **45 days**.
- **AG notification:** Required if breach affects **500+ Florida residents**; same 30-day (extendable to 45-day) deadline, notice to the Florida Department of Legal Affairs (AG).
- **Consumer reporting agency (CRA) notice:** Required if notifying **1,000+ individuals at one time**; notify nationwide CRAs without unreasonable delay, including timing/distribution/content of consumer notices.
- **Payment card + security code as "personal information":** Yes — statute explicitly covers a financial account or credit/debit card number **in combination with any required security code, access code, or password** needed to access the account.
- **Encryption safe harbor:** Yes — "personal information" excludes data that is encrypted, secured, or otherwise rendered unusable/unidentifiable (subject to the encryption key itself not being compromised).
- **Penalties:** Civil penalty up to **$1,000/day for the first 30 days**, then up to **$50,000 per subsequent 30-day period**, capped at **$500,000 per breach**. Also enforceable as an unfair/deceptive trade practice under FDUTPA (§ 501.207).
- **Private right of action:** **No** — enforcement is by the Department of Legal Affairs (AG) only; not confirmed to give individuals a direct FIPA claim.

## 2. Consumer-Facing Payment / Billing Notices

- **Automatic renewal law:** Fla. Stat. § 501.165 (service contracts with an automatic-renewal provision).
  - Applies to contracts with an automatic renewal provision that extends the contract **more than 1 month** and, when renewed, keeps it in effect **more than 6 months** from initiation.
  - Renewal provision must be disclosed **clearly and conspicuously** in the contract.
  - For contracts with an initial term of **12+ months** that auto-renew for more than 1 month: seller must send written/electronic notice **no less than 30 days and no more than 60 days** before the cancellation deadline.
  - Consumer must be able to cancel **in the same manner** used to accept the contract.
  - Violation renders the automatic-renewal provision **void and unenforceable**.
- **Price-increase notice / recurring-charge notice generally:** No standalone Florida statute found requiring advance notice of a price increase outside the § 501.165 auto-renewal notice window. Not confirmed — verify with counsel for sector-specific rules (e.g., insurance, utilities).
- **Cancellation-confirmation notice:** Not confirmed — § 501.165 requires an equivalent cancellation *method*, but no located requirement for a written cancellation-confirmation notice.

## 3. State-Specific EFT/ACH Notice Requirements (beyond Reg E)

- No Florida-specific statute located that adds notice obligations for preauthorized/recurring EFT or ACH debits beyond federal Reg E (12 C.F.R. § 1005.10) / EFTA § 913 (15 U.S.C. § 1693e). **Not confirmed — verify with counsel.**
