# District of Columbia — Notification Law Reference

**Disclaimer:** Internal compliance reference only. Not legal advice. Verify with counsel before relying on any item marked "Not confirmed."

## 1. Data Breach Notification Law

- **Statute:** DC Code § 28-3851 (definitions) and § 28-3852 (notification duty), as amended by the Security Breach Protection Amendment Act of 2020 (D.C. Law 23-98); remedies at § 28-3852.02.
- **Trigger:** Any person/entity conducting business in DC that owns or licenses computerized data including personal information **discovers a breach of the security of the system**.
- **Consumer notice deadline:** **No fixed day count.** Statute requires notice "in the most expedient time possible and without unreasonable delay," consistent with law-enforcement needs and measures to determine breach scope.
- **AG notice threshold/deadline:** If **50 or more** DC residents are affected, must promptly provide **written notice to the DC Office of the Attorney General**, in the most expedient manner possible and without unreasonable delay, and **no later than** when notice is provided to residents.
- **Consumer reporting agency notice:** Not confirmed as a distinct DC statutory requirement in the sections reviewed (unlike WI's 1,000-person CRA trigger); verify current § 28-3852 text for any CRA notice clause added by later amendment.
- **Payment card + security code covered as "personal information"?** **Yes** — § 28-3851 defines covered data (combined with name/identifier) to include "credit card number or debit card number, or any other number or code ... such as an identification number, security code, access code, or password that allows access to or use of an individual's financial or credit account." Also covers SSN/gov't ID numbers, medical/mental-health treatment info, genetic information, health insurance info, biometric data, and username/email + password or security question.
- **Encryption/redaction safe harbor:** **No encryption safe harbor found** in § 28-3852 as reviewed — treat as **not confirmed / likely absent**; verify current statute text directly.
- **Identity theft protection service mandate:** Where the breach includes or is reasonably believed to include an SSN or taxpayer ID, the entity **must offer affected DC residents no-cost identity theft protection services for at least 18 months** (§ 28-3852.02).
- **Penalties / enforcement:** Violations may be treated as "unfair and deceptive trade practices" under the DC Consumer Protection Procedures Act (CPPA).
- **Private right of action:** **Yes, via the CPPA** — consumers harmed may sue for **treble damages or $1,500 per violation (whichever is greater)**, plus actual damages and attorney's fees. (Not stated as a standalone right within § 28-3852 itself; it flows through CPPA classification.)

## 2. Consumer-Facing Payment/Billing Notices

- **Price-increase notice:** Not confirmed as a standalone general requirement outside the automatic-renewal regime below.
- **Automatic Renewal Protections Act (ARPA):** DC Code Title 28A, Ch. 2 (§§ 28A-201 et seq.), notice/penalties at **§ 28A-203**.
  - Contracts with an **initial term ≥ 12 months** that auto-renew for **≥ 1 month**: business must send written notice of the upcoming renewal **no fewer than 30 days and no more than 60 days** before the cancellation deadline, for the **first renewal and annually thereafter**.
  - Notice must clearly/conspicuously disclose: that the contract will auto-renew unless canceled, the cost for the renewal term, the cancellation deadline, and the cancellation method(s).
  - Delivery: first-class mail, email, or (if consumer specifically authorized) another easily accessible method (text/app); email notices must include active weblinks enabling cancellation.
  - **Free trials:** if a free trial (≥1 month) auto-converts to a paid auto-renewing term, business must notify the consumer of the auto-renewal **at least 15 and no more than 30 days before the trial ends**, and obtain **affirmative consent** before charging.
  - **Enforcement/private right of action:** A violation is a violation of the CPPA — consumer may sue for **treble damages or $1,500/violation** (whichever greater) plus attorney's fees; AG may also investigate/enforce (injunctions, restitution, civil penalties).
  - **Good-faith safe harbor:** violations are not actionable if the business had written compliance procedures in place and promptly credited/refunded amounts billed due to a mistaken renewal.
- **Cancellation-confirmation notice:** Not a separately named requirement found; the cancellation mechanics/deadline disclosures above function as the operative consumer protection. Verify with counsel whether OAG guidance imposes an explicit post-cancellation confirmation duty.

## 3. EFT/ACH Notice Requirements Beyond Federal Reg E

- **Not confirmed** — no DC-specific statute found imposing preauthorized-transfer or change-in-terms notice requirements for recurring consumer debits beyond federal Reg E (12 CFR Part 1005). DC Code § 28:4A-206 (UCC Article 4A) addresses transmission of payment orders in wholesale funds-transfer systems, not consumer recurring-debit notices, and does not apply where federal EFTA governs the transfer.
